# Sustainability Strategy

The BOSC Community Library should remain free to access while still having a realistic plan for long-term maintenance. The recommended model is a foundation-driven structure supported by public grants, university partnerships, and transparent donations.

## Red Hat Service-Based Model

The Red Hat model earns revenue by selling professional services around open-source software. For the BOSC Community Library, this could include paid training, implementation support for schools, documentation workshops, and compliance audits for public agencies.

This model is useful because it does not close the source material. The repository remains public, while institutions pay for reliability, onboarding, and expert support. It also creates a practical path for local consultants and graduates to earn income by helping organizations adopt open-source practices.

The limitation is that a service business needs constant delivery capacity. If the project depends too heavily on a small group of maintainers, service requests can distract from community governance and content quality.

## Foundation-Driven Model

A foundation-driven model places the library under a neutral governance body such as an education technology foundation, university consortium, or public-interest nonprofit. Funding can come from grants, donations, sponsorships, and institutional memberships.

This model fits the BOSC Community Library because the project serves public education rather than one vendor's commercial roadmap. A foundation can publish budgets, sponsor maintenance sprints, support translation work, and protect the project from capture by a single company.

The limitation is administrative overhead. A foundation needs clear policies, fiscal reporting, and a board or steering committee. Without active governance, the structure can become symbolic rather than useful.

## Recommended Approach

BOSC should adopt a hybrid model: foundation-led governance with optional paid services delivered by certified community partners. The foundation would own the public roadmap, funding transparency, and community health metrics. Service partners would provide training, migration support, and local deployment assistance.

This approach protects the commons while allowing commercial activity around the project. It also matches public-sector expectations: transparent governance, auditable funding, and no vendor lock-in.
